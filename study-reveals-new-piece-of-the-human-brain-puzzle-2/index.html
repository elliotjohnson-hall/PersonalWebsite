Study reveals new piece of the human brain puzzle

The question of ‘what makes us human?’ has puzzled philosophers and scientists alike for millennia. Literature and language, art, reasoned debate - are just a few of the things that set us apart from the rest of the animal kingdom as humans. The unifying factor in all these activities is our complex brain.

Now, new research has revealed one way in which our brains differ from those of our close relatives. The paper, published in Nature, emerged from UCSF in California, and reveals human brain is unique within the first trimester.

Rodents and humans have incredibly different brains. Yet most prior research used rodents to try and understand the complexity of human brains. Understandably, studying live human brains in a lab is ethically prohibited. Modern techniques have now enabled scientists to grow human brain cells in a dish to explore how the human brain differs from animals.

Cells multiply by dividing: one cell splits into two. During this division, a cell’s offspring can take on new characteristics. Following an individual cell’s descendants is relatively simple. However, the problem of scale remained; human brains are made of tens of billions of cells which mature and interact to give rise to our human experience.

The researchers’ challenge was how to track the offspring of many cells simultaneously. By developing a new way to ‘barcode’ individual cells, Delgado and colleagues were able to follow thousands of cells and their descendants over long time periods.

In rodents, two types of brain cells - excitatory and inhibitory are born in different places within the brain, from different cell families. In contrast, both excitatory and inhibitory human brain cells are born from the same cells in the same location.

Overall, this work has revealed another way in which the human brain is set apart from those of other species.

In rodents, excitatory (green) and inhibitory (red) brain cells are born from two different types of cell (pink & yellow). In humans, these cells are born from a single cell type.

Delgado, R.N., Allen, D.E., Keefe, M.G. et al. Individual human cortical progenitors can produce excitatory and inhibitory neurons. Nature 601, 397–403 (2022).
